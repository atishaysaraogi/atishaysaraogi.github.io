Holdfast

Problem: Regular walkers can’t be pulled on to stand up without tipping over, which meant our client needed an additional person to help pull to stand.

Project: Design Thinking and Communication, for the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab

Solution: The Holdfast uses glass-lifting suction cups to allow the user to pull to stand without an additional helper.

Team: Atishay Saraogi, Michael Gooding, Zakariah Lee, Neven Vaduthala

We narrowed the scope for a single user

  • We interviewed user's physical therpaists (PTs) to understand that he used the walker at home and at the AbilityLab, narrowing our use environments.
  • User finds it easier to pull than to push, therefore our solution must be stable enough to pull on.
  • User weighs less than 200 lbs, 5x factor of safety recommended on medical devices.
  • Existing solutions were too heavy or bulky, others tipped over.

The floors were smooth, why not suction cups?

I came up with the idea of using suction cups after we tested out weights, counter-balances, support structures, and more. They offered a light yet sturdy solution with 800 lbs of force that worked with the smooth floors of the AbilityLab and the user's room.

A workable addition to our walker

  • I coordinated with 3 other teams to make sure our attachment worked with the walker.
  • The physical therapists approved the walker and the factor of safety was sufficient.
  • User’s mother (primary caregiver) found operating the Holdfast more convenient.

Holdfast as an attachment to the walker

I learnt to design for specific needs.

I learnt about working with medical equipment and how failure modes and factors of safety should be approached. I also grew to appreciate how devices need to work for people with vastly different capabilities and requirements. I also developed an appreciation for inclusive design.
